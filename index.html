The Problem

Most D2C brands are investing in the wrong fix.

You increase ad spend — but the bottleneck is retention. You redesign creatives — but the issue is offer structure. You switch agencies — but the real problem is narrative breakdown across your funnel.

The most common reason D2C brands plateau is not execution failure. It's diagnostic failure — acting on the wrong hypothesis, repeatedly.

Clariva operates at that layer: identifying what is actually constraining your growth, with specificity, before any solution is prescribed.

Clariva is not an agency. We do not run ads, manage campaigns, or create content. Our work is purely diagnostic — and that separation is intentional. It is the reason the diagnosis can be trusted.

Key Problem

Revenue grew 17× over six years — but marketing spend grew at nearly the same rate. Every year required the same proportional ad investment just to maintain growth. In a high-repurchase skincare category, this is only possible if customers are not returning on their own. The brand was renting attention, not building equity.

Revenue Growth vs. Ad Spend % of Revenue — FY2020 to FY2025
A compounding brand shows declining ad spend % over time. Mamaearth's never declined. Industry benchmark: ~17.5%. Mamaearth held at 37–42% for six consecutive years.
The signal: If retention were working, spend % would trend down as repeat customers reduce the acquisition burden. It never did — confirming a structural, not cyclical, retention failure.
Primary Constraint: Retention Architecture Failure

Mamaearth built a world-class acquisition engine and a near-absent retention system. The ad creative mix — 55% offer-driven (BOGO, discounts) — self-selected for price-sensitive customers who don't return at full price, compounding the retention problem at the acquisition stage itself. 70% gross margin confirmed the product was never the issue. The breakdown lived entirely in the marketing system below gross profit.

Clariva's Diagnostic

Your marketing spend is not declining as a percentage of revenue despite strong growth — you are not building a compounding asset. Before scaling acquisition further: identify why existing customers are not returning, and build one systematic post-purchase retention mechanism. The signal was readable two to three years before the financial impact became undeniable.

Key Problem

boAt ran a lean, efficient audio business for eight years — 3.4% of revenue on ads, consistently profitable. Then in one year (FY23), ad spend jumped 4.3× to fund a smartwatch category push. Revenue grew only 18% in response. The following year, wearables revenue fell 40%. One strategic decision broke eight years of profitability because the brand's competitive moat — lifestyle cool — did not transfer to a category where buyers evaluate features, health accuracy, and software.

Revenue vs. Ad Spend — The Year Everything Changed
FY22: ₹99 Cr on ads, ₹68.7 Cr profit. FY23: ₹428 Cr on ads, ₹129.4 Cr loss. One strategic decision. One year. Unlike Mamaearth's gradual treadmill, boAt's break was abrupt.
The signal: Ad spend grew 4.3× while revenue grew only 18%. This is not a gradual efficiency decline — it is a single-year misallocation driven by a category bet that failed.
Primary Constraint: Category-Capability Misalignment

boAt's growth constraint was not a marketing execution problem — it was a strategic positioning problem. The brand's lifestyle equity in audio did not transfer to smartwatches, where Noise and Fire-Boltt won decisively on product and software. Wearables went from ₹901 Cr to ₹330 Cr over two years. The underlying retention weakness (28% repeat rate vs 40–50% benchmark) was always present but became critical once acquisition costs spiked — there was no loyal base to absorb it.

Clariva's Diagnostic

Before allocating capital to a new category, audit whether your competitive advantage transfers. In smartwatches, purchase decisions are driven by features, software, and health accuracy — not lifestyle branding. Your current moat does not apply. Additionally, your repeat purchase rate is below the healthy D2C benchmark. Fix the retention foundation in audio first; then enter new categories with a capability plan, not just a marketing budget.
